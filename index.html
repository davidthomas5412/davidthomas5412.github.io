A Measurement of Excess Antenna Temperature at 4080 Mc/s.
Penzias, A. A. and Wilson, R. W.
1965
Penzias and Wilson present how they calculated a 3.5K background temperature with horn reflector antenna. The authors practice self control and leave the explanation of the cause of their observation to the companion paper above.
The Origin of Chemical Elements
R.A. Alpher, H. Bethe, G. Gamow
1948
This paper introduces what has come to be called big bang nucleosynthesis. The authors use nuclear cross sections and their relationship to atomic number to fit a predicted abundance curve against observed chemical abundances. The agreement of the fit suggests that neutron capture is responsible for the hydrogen and helium in the universe. At the time of this paper it was not known that there was no stable isotope with an atomic mass of 5 which would have prevented synthesis of heavier elements than lithium. Eventually the discovery of stellar nucleosynthesis would explain the origin of the heavier elements.
A Relation Between Distance And Radial Velocity Among Extra-Galactic Nebulae
Edwin Hubble
1929
Hubble provides strong evidence to support the existing hypothesis of a velocity-distance relationship (which would eventually be named Hubble's Law: v=HD). He confirms this relationship by fitting a set of stars and clusters (via mean) with distances measured (I presume by cepheid variable stars) and another with distances approximated by general luminosity - both are in close agreement and the residuals are easily within experimental error.
Periods of 25 Variable Stars in the Small Magellanic Cloud
Leavitt, H. S. and Pickering, E. C.
1912
In Periods of 25 Variable Stars in the Small Magellanic Cloud, Leavitt and Pickering suggest a logarithmic relationship between the period and luminosity of cepheid variable stars. In my response I validate that the logarithmic fit performs best and provide a slight improvement by taking the mid apparent magnitude.

Other Recently Read Papers

A Practical Guide to Training Restricted Boltzmann Machines
Geoffrey Hinton
2010
This paper summarizes Hinton's work on using contrastive divergence to train large networks of restricted boltzmann machies. He clearly explains how contrastive divergence, which involves a small, fixed number of sampling iterations per sample, improves on Gibbs sampling, which runs until convergence. I highly recommend it for anyone interested in employing deep learning.

TensorFlow: Large-Scale Machine Learning on Heterogeneous Distributed Systems
Google Research
2015
Provides an overview of tensor flow - Google's large scale machine learning framework - and the reasoning behind key design decisions. I was impressive that they will automatically add gradients to your computation graph, by their greedy algorithm which allocates the computation graph across machines, and their use of lossy compression. This project will likely join the ranks of HDFS, MapReduce, Spark, Storm, and HBase as the next widespread big data platform.

An effectiveness measure for software clustering algorithms
Zhihua Wen and Vassilios Tzerpos
2004
The authors present a significantly improved metric (MojoFM) for evaluating different clustering algorithms on a labelled dataset. I have found this metric to be valuable for selecting the ideal clustering algorithm for a specific problem or context.

Reducing the Dimensionality of Data with Neural Networks
G. E. Hinton and R. R. Salakhutdinov
2006
Extremely concise explanation of RBM autoencoders. Great reference for demonstrating the power of autoencoder dimensionality reduction over PCA in binary vector datasets.
